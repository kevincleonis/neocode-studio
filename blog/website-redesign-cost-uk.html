How Much Does a Website Redesign Cost in the UK? (2026 Guide)

The honest answer is: it depends. But that is not helpful, so let us break down the real numbers, what drives them, and — more importantly — how to think about website investment in terms of return, not just cost.

If you have been Googling "how much does a website cost UK" you have probably seen answers ranging from £500 to £50,000. Both of those numbers are real, and the gap between them can be baffling. This guide will help you understand where your business sits on that spectrum and what you should actually be paying.

The Quick Overview

Option Typical Cost Best For
DIY Website Builder £0 - £500 Hobby projects, very early-stage ideas
Freelance Designer £800 - £3,000 Small businesses needing a basic online presence
Specialist Studio £2,000 - £8,000 Businesses serious about generating leads and sales
Full-Service Agency £10,000 - £50,000+ Large brands, complex e-commerce, custom applications

Option 1: DIY Website Builders

Platforms like Wix, Squarespace, and Shopify let you build a website yourself for very little money. Plans start from £10-15 per month, and you can technically have a site live within an afternoon.

The appeal is obvious: it is cheap and fast. But there are significant trade-offs. Templates look generic, customisation is limited, and most importantly, these platforms do not optimise for conversions. A pretty template and a website that actually generates leads are two very different things.

DIY builders work well if you just need a basic online presence — a digital business card, essentially. But if your website is a primary source of customers, the limitations become expensive very quickly in terms of lost revenue.

Option 2: Freelance Designers

Hiring a freelance web designer in the UK typically costs between £800 and £3,000 for a small business website (5-10 pages). You will find freelancers on platforms like Upwork, Fiverr, or through personal recommendations.

The quality varies enormously. Some freelancers are brilliant — former agency designers who went independent. Others are hobbyists charging low rates and delivering template-based sites with minimal customisation.

What to watch out for

  • Ask to see their portfolio and speak to previous clients
  • Clarify what is included: design only, or also content writing, SEO setup, and mobile optimisation?
  • Understand the ongoing costs — who handles hosting, updates, and maintenance?
  • Get a clear timeline with milestones

Option 3: Specialist Studios

This is where you start getting strategic thinking alongside design. A specialist studio (like us, at Neocode Studio) does not just make your website look good — they build it to achieve specific business outcomes.

At the £2,000-£8,000 range, you should expect:

  • A proper discovery phase to understand your business, customers, and goals
  • Custom design tailored to your brand (not a tweaked template)
  • Conversion-optimised layouts based on real data and best practices
  • Mobile-first responsive design
  • On-page SEO setup so Google can find you
  • Speed optimisation for fast loading times
  • Integration with your booking system, CRM, or payment processor

This is typically the sweet spot for small to medium UK businesses that rely on their website for lead generation. The investment pays for itself quickly when your site starts converting visitors into customers.

Option 4: Full-Service Agencies

Large agencies in London, Manchester, or Birmingham charge £10,000-£50,000+ for a website project. This includes extensive research, UX design, custom development, content strategy, and sometimes ongoing marketing support.

This tier makes sense for larger brands with complex requirements: multi-language sites, advanced e-commerce with thousands of products, custom web applications, or major corporate rebrandings. For most small businesses, it is overkill.

What Actually Affects the Price?

Regardless of who you hire, these factors drive the cost up or down:

  • Number of pages: A 5-page brochure site costs less than a 30-page site with a blog, portfolio, and member area
  • Custom functionality: Online booking, payment processing, customer portals, and integrations add complexity
  • Content creation: Professional copywriting and photography are separate skills that add value (and cost)
  • E-commerce: Selling products online requires catalogue management, checkout flows, and payment gateway setup
  • Ongoing support: Some agencies include 12 months of maintenance; others charge extra
  • Timeline: Need it in 2 weeks instead of 6? Rush jobs cost more

The ROI Perspective

Here is the question that actually matters: what is the cost of not investing in your website?

Let us run some simple numbers. Say your current website gets 500 visitors per month and converts at 1% — that is 5 enquiries. A properly optimised site might convert at 3-5%. At 3%, that is 15 enquiries instead of 5. If each customer is worth £500, you have just gone from £2,500 to £7,500 in monthly revenue.

That is £5,000 per month in additional revenue — £60,000 per year. Suddenly, a £3,000-£5,000 website investment does not look expensive. It looks like the best business decision you could make.

The cheapest website is rarely the best value. The best value is the one that pays for itself — and then keeps paying you back, month after month.

How to Choose the Right Option

Be honest about what your website needs to do for your business. If it is your primary lead generation tool, investing in a site that actually converts is not a luxury — it is a necessity.

Get multiple quotes, but do not just compare prices. Compare what is included, ask about their process, look at results they have achieved for similar businesses, and check whether they understand conversion optimisation — not just design.
